Opinion Canes • Week 6 Reaction

If that’s their push, what exactly are the Canes building toward?

They led 2–1 midway through the second. Then they faded — and the collapse wasn’t chaos. It was absence of pace, pressure, and purpose.

By Mark Ellison, Columnist
•
Thursday Opinion
•
12-week season

Midway through the second period, the Canes were up 2–1. They weren’t dominating, but they were leading — and against Pink Whitney, that’s supposed to matter.

Instead, it vanished. Pink Whitney scored three unanswered and turned a winnable night into a 4–2 loss. The Canes didn’t lose this game on a bounce, a whistle, or a freak moment. They lost it because they got slower.

The lead wasn’t stolen. It was surrendered.

Missing bodies isn’t the same as missing identity

Yes — Ryan Kowal, Jason Powers, and Chad Gerdes were out. Those absences matter in specific ways: physical tone, forecheck chaos, and shift stability.

But if the team’s identity collapses because three players are missing, then the identity isn’t stable. A contender absorbs absences. It doesn’t disappear.

The forward group looked slow — and worse, passive

The clearest problem wasn’t tactical. It was visible. The forward group looked extremely slow for long stretches, with limited pressure and almost no consistent forecheck. Outside of Andrew Sands — once again the only forward consistently forcing offense — there wasn’t enough push to tilt the ice back.

That’s the part that should scare the Canes. Not that they lost. Not that it was 4–2. That their response to adversity looked like fading rather than accelerating.

The pattern is no longer subtle

Two straight losses to the same opponent. A blown lead. A third period where the game slipped away without a fight. For weeks, the Canes have leaned on defense and structure to keep games close — and when they’re chasing, the offense arrives late, in bursts, instead of as a steady presence.

Why does this team only look dangerous when it’s desperate?

If urgency only shows up when trailing, it isn’t urgency. It’s panic.

The fans aren’t wrong to feel it

You can dismiss frustration as overreaction. You can wave off the talk-radio impulse to “nuke the team.” But when a team looks visibly slower after holding a lead — when the forecheck feels optional and the push never arrives — frustration isn’t hysteria. It’s observation.

The Canes aren’t getting blown out. They’re getting outpaced. That’s harder to fix than a mistake. Because it’s not a moment — it’s a posture.

So what now?

This is a 12-week season. The trade deadline is Week 8. There isn’t time to wait for belief to return by itself. The Canes don’t need a rebuild. But they do need a decision — about whether they’re playing to protect margins, or playing to create separation.

Because the version of the team that showed up after that second-period lead won’t win in April. And it won’t calm anyone before then.
