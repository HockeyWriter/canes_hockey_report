Where the Canes stand after Week 3: Better than the record

The Canes are working their way into the top half of the table, but the real story is how close the margins have been — and why a 1–0 loss can be both warning sign and reassurance.

By Rinkside Staff
• Updated Feb 2026
• 4 min read
Key takeaways
  • The Canes are competitive every night, backed by steady goaltending and elite effort.
  • They’re generating chances — but finishing remains the difference in one-goal games.
  • David Marples and Andrew Sands continue to drive play; Kenneth Lee is the quiet stabilizer.

Three weeks into the season, the Canes sit in the upper half of the table, but the numbers don’t quite capture the story unfolding on the ice. This is a team that looks organized, skates well, and rarely gets outworked — and yet it’s also a team living on the thin edge where one bounce becomes a headline.

Monday’s 1–0 loss to the Owls, a result not yet reflected in the standings, felt like a microcosm of the early season: long stretches of pressure, quality looks created, and a game that stayed within reach all night — until it didn’t.

“The Canes are close. The question is whether they can turn close into points before the table tightens.”
— League beat notes, Week 3

The standings say one thing — the games say another

The Canes have shown they can score in bunches, but they’ve also proven vulnerable to low-event games where execution matters more than volume. Against the Owls, they generated pressure and pushed late, but the finishing touch never arrived.

That’s not a structural problem — it’s a conversion problem. The process is there. The results have been streaky. In a league where most games are decided by one or two goals, that margin isn’t a detail; it’s the season.

Why the Canes are still a factor

Start with the foundation. Michael Brown continues to keep games tight, giving the Canes a chance even on nights when offense is hard to come by. Andrew Sands remains the engine — skating hard, getting pucks back, and forcing sequences to happen. Kenneth Lee quietly controls the tempo with smart reads and tape-to-tape touches.

And then there’s the blue line: David Marples is still the best puck handler on the ice in most matchups, driving offense from the back end in ways few teams can replicate. When the Canes look dangerous, he’s often at the center of it.

What’s holding them back

The early-season concern is simple: finishing. The Canes are creating chances — off forechecks, off entries, off point shots — but they haven’t consistently cashed them in. That puts pressure on every defensive mistake, because the margin for error disappears when one goal has to feel like three.

The good news is this isn’t about effort. It’s about timing. If a couple of the grinders get one to drop, the narrative flips quickly. For now, the Canes remain exactly what they look like on the ice: a hard-working team that’s closer than the standings suggest — and dangerous if the offense wakes up.
