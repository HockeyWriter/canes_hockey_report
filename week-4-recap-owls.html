GAME RECAP Week 4 • Canes

Canes do everything but score in 1–0 loss to Owls

Despite sustained pressure, clean entries, and multiple Grade-A looks, the Canes couldn’t break through in a one-goal grinder.

By Rinkside Staff
• Feb 2, 2026
• 4 min read
Key takeaways
  • David Marbles drove offense from the back end with repeated coast-to-coast carries.
  • The Canes generated sustained pressure but ran into a locked-in Owls goalie.
  • An early unlucky bounce was the only thing that separated the teams.

The Canes’ Week 4 matchup against the Owls was decided almost as soon as it began — not by structure, not by dominance, but by an awkward bounce that slipped past Michael Brown just two minutes in.

From that point on, the ice tilted heavily in one direction. The Canes controlled possession, entered the zone with speed, and forced the Owls to defend for long stretches. The scoreboard, however, never moved.

“This wasn’t a game where the Canes were chasing — it was a game where they kept knocking and the door never opened.”
— Rinkside notes, Week 4

Short bench, strong response

The Canes were already thin heading into the night. Defenseman Garrett Harry and winger Jason Powers were out with injuries, while David Marples remains sidelined with a broken toe and is expected to miss another four to six weeks.

The forward group featured Ryan Alvarado at center on the top line with captain Jeffrey Golsch on the left and Ryan Kowal on the right. The second unit saw Thomas Pucci in the middle, flanked by Jackson Brown and Andrew Sands on the wing.

Marbles tilts the ice

If there was one player who looked capable of breaking the game open, it was David Marbles. Time and again, he carried the puck coast-to-coast, slicing through neutral-zone coverage and getting shots on goal from dangerous areas.

Marbles wasn’t just moving the puck — he was creating offense by himself. Each rush forced the Owls to reset, and each shot added to the growing sense that the Canes were one bounce away from leveling the game.

Sands drives the forecheck

Up front, Andrew Sands was buzzing all night. He won races, pressured defenders, and created multiple high-quality chances off the cycle. The Canes weren’t settling for perimeter looks; they were generating legitimate offense.

The problem wasn’t chance quality. It was the man in the crease.

What it means

Nights like this tend to linger. The Canes did enough to win, controlled the game for long stretches, and still came away with nothing. In a league defined by thin margins, one early bounce erased forty minutes of work.

The process was strong. The result wasn’t. And the standings don’t care which one you liked better.
